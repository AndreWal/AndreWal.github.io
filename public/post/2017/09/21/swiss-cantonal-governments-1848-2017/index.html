Comparative Politics

Swiss Cantonal Governments 1848-2017

André Walter

Tags: Swiss Politics; History; Data

I’m currently working with Patrick Emmenegger on creating a database on the ideological composition of cantonal governments. We were able to compile a time series for most cantons starting in the mid 19th century. A complete Time-Series Cross-Section is available for all cantons for 1894 onwards (except Appenzell Inner-Rhodes which starts 1928 and, of course, Jura in 1978).

Below, you can find the first exploratory results. The first plot displays the smoothed government volatility for 24 cantons (excl. AI & JU), i.e. whether a new party got a seat in government or a party was voted out of office. The increase in volatility in the first half of the 20th century can be largely attributed to the Social Democrats and the introduction of Proportional Representation for parliamentry elections (which also affected the composition of government despite the direct election of government; Fribourg came last in 1921). However, the increase in 70s seems more pronounce. Smoothing appears to be a bit misleading, the “real” takeoff is only in the 90s.

Who lost over time? As the next plot for all cantons shows, timing and pace might differ but, unsurprisingly, the radicals and the catholic conservatives are the main losers. In contrast, the Social Democrats largely stagnated with an average seat share of about 20% since the 70s. The Swiss Peoples Party has been also gaining more and more seats in cantonal governments throughout the 20th century but at an extrem slow pace.

We hope to have a first working paper with a description of the data, sources and interesting descriptives by the end of the year. Stay tuned!
